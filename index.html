Visa Requirements for Australians

Australians require a visa to enter Iran. Options under normal circumstances:

  • E-Visa (Electronic): Apply via Iran's MFA portal. Receive a reference number; obtain visa stamp on arrival. Process takes 2–4 weeks. Cost approx. €75–100.
  • Visa on Arrival: Available at Imam Khomeini International Airport (Tehran). Fees approx. €145. Not available to Australians in all circumstances — pre-approval recommended.
  • Embassy Visa: Apply through an Iranian embassy with passport submission. Most reliable option.
Critical Restriction for Australians Following the diplomat expulsion in August 2025 and the current conflict, visa issuance for Australian passport holders may be suspended. Verify directly before any application.

6. Drone Laws — Your Question Answered

DO NOT BRING
🚫
Do NOT Bring Your Drone
Confiscation guaranteed. Arrest probable. Espionage charges possible.

The Law

The Civil Aviation Organization of Iran (CAOI) regulates drones. While drone use is technically regulated (not banned), the reality for foreign visitors — especially Australians — is far more dangerous:

  • Drones will be confiscated at customs — this is the standard outcome for tourists attempting to bring drones into Iran
  • Any drone over 300g requires a permit from Iranian authorities
  • Flying in Tehran is explicitly prohibited — the capital is a restricted zone
  • Flying near military zones, nuclear sites, police stations is a criminal offence
  • Foreign visitors caught with unpermitted drones face arrest on suspicion of espionage
  • Multiple incidents exist of tourists being held for months on spy allegations from drone use

In an Active War Zone — The Stakes Are Higher

🔴 Extreme Danger: Zero Tolerance During Conflict Iran is currently under military strikes and on maximum security alert. Any drone — even a tiny consumer quadcopter — will be treated as a potential hostile surveillance device. Bringing a drone into Iran right now would almost certainly result in immediate arrest, confiscation of all your equipment, and potentially lengthy imprisonment on espionage charges. Do not even consider it.

Permit Process (Academic — Not Recommended)

In normal peacetime, a foreign visitor would need to: obtain written approval from the Ministry of Defence and Armed Forces; register the drone with CAOI; obtain a local sponsor/operator permit. This process typically takes months, is rarely granted to foreign nationals, and is currently suspended during the conflict.

TripTrust Verdict: Leave your drone at home. Do not attempt to bring it across the Iranian border under any circumstances, now or in the near future.

7. Getting Around Tehran

⚠ Conflict Impact on Transport Infrastructure including roads and transport hubs may be damaged. Airport operations may be disrupted. Check real-time updates before any movement.

Metro (Tehran Metro)

Tehran has an extensive metro system (9 lines) covering most of the city. Affordable (~5,000–10,000 Toman/trip), safe, and air-conditioned. Women-only carriages at front and rear. Excellent for business districts. Note: separate carriages for men and women are recommended.

Taxis

Official orange taxis (metered) and shared taxis (savari) operate throughout Tehran. Always negotiate fare before boarding for private taxis, or insist on the meter.

Snapp (Iranian Ride-Share)

Snapp is Iran's equivalent of Uber and is widely used in Tehran. Requires a local SIM card and Iranian phone number to register. Your hotel concierge can often arrange this. Fixed fares, GPS tracked, generally safer than street taxis.

Domestic Flights

Iran Air, Mahan Air, and Iran Aseman Airlines operate domestic routes. Given the current conflict, all domestic aviation may be disrupted or suspended. Airspace may be closed to civilian traffic.

Driving

International driving permits are not valid in Iran. Iranian driving standards are erratic. Not recommended for business visitors. Use hired drivers from your hotel.

11. Cultural & Religious Rules

Dress Code

Mandatory for all visitors — Iranian law requires adherence to Islamic dress code.

  • Women: Hijab mandatory in public — hair covering (scarf/headscarf), loose-fitting clothing covering arms and legs. Manteau (long coat) recommended for business.
  • Men: Long trousers required. Short-sleeved shirts acceptable but long sleeves preferred in formal settings. No shorts in public.
  • Business attire should be conservative and professional.

Alcohol

Alcohol is completely illegal in Iran Possession or consumption of alcohol by non-Muslims is technically illegal and can result in fines or flogging (rarely applied to foreigners but theoretically possible). Luxury hotels do not serve alcohol. Plan accordingly.

Friday Closures

Friday is the Islamic day of rest. Most government offices, banks, and many businesses are closed on Fridays. Some businesses also close Thursday afternoons. Plan business meetings for Saturday–Wednesday.

Business Etiquette

  • Relationships are paramount — invest time in personal rapport before business
  • Appointments should be made 4–6 weeks in advance; confirm on arrival
  • Business cards: present and receive with both hands
  • Gender dynamics: male visitors should wait for women to extend hand first
  • Ta'arof (ritual politeness/offering) is a cultural cornerstone — always decline offers twice before accepting
  • Gift-giving is common; avoid alcohol, pork, or politically sensitive items
  • Meetings often run long and relationship-driven; patience is essential
  • Punctuality: be punctual yourself; your Iranian counterparts may not be

Ramadan (March 25 — Final Days)

Your trip begins during the final days of Ramadan 2026. Eating, drinking, or smoking in public during daylight is prohibited by law — fines apply. Hotel restaurants serve food. Eid al-Fitr (end of Ramadan) will fall around March 30 — a major holiday. Many businesses will be closed.

Digital & Communication Rules

  • Many Western social media platforms blocked (Instagram, Facebook, Twitter/X, WhatsApp)
  • VPNs are widely used by Iranians but technically illegal
  • Encrypted messaging may attract attention
  • Photography of government buildings, military, protests strictly prohibited
